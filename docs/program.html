Theoretical Framework

A multiphase Eulerian-Eulerian approach has been adopted in this study, in which both particles and fluid are modeled as continuous phases. The particle size is assumed to be constant. The propellant is modeled as an additional phase, and the propellant regression is governed by the burning rate law. The gas phase mixture is compressible and its thermodynamic state is governed by the ideal gas equation of state, and the particle phase is treated as an incompressible phase with constant density. The combustion process is modeled as a single-step process producing only major gas-phase product species and particles, which enter the combustion chamber. The species diffusion processes in the rocket motor are neglected, considering high velocities in the rocket motor. Viscous effects in the fluid flow are considered and the no-slip condition is enforced along the walls of the rocket motor. The gas is assumed to be chemically frozen during expansion and species diffusion is ignored, thus, the species mass conservation equations for gas-phase species are not solved.

Governing Equations:

The mass conservation equations of gas and particle phases are given by:

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\alpha_k \rho_k) + \nabla \cdot (\alpha_k \rho_k \boldsymbol{u}_k) = \dot{m}'''_{prop \rightarrow k} \tag{1} $$

where $\alpha_k$, $\rho_k$, and $\boldsymbol{u}_k$ are the volume fraction, density, and velocity of the $k^{\text{th}}$ phase, respectively. The source term due to mass transfer from propellant to $k^{\text{th}}$ phase is denoted as $\dot{m}'''_{prop \rightarrow k}$. The momentum conservation equations for the gas phase and particle phase are given by:

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\alpha_g \rho_g \boldsymbol{u}_g) + \nabla \cdot (\alpha_g \rho_g \boldsymbol{u}_g \boldsymbol{u}_g) = -\alpha_g \nabla P + \nabla \cdot (\alpha_g \boldsymbol{\tau}_g) + \boldsymbol{M}_{prop \rightarrow g} + \boldsymbol{f}_{D,g} \tag{2} $$

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\alpha_p \rho_p \boldsymbol{u}_p) + \nabla \cdot (\alpha_p \rho_p \boldsymbol{u}_p \boldsymbol{u}_p) = -\alpha_p \nabla P + \boldsymbol{M}_{prop \rightarrow p} + \boldsymbol{f}_{D,p} \tag{3} $$

where $P$ is the common pressure experienced by all the phases, $\tau_k$ is the shear stress in the $k^{\text{th}}$ phase, and $f_{D,k}$ is the drag force experienced by the $k^{\text{th}}$ phase. The subscripts $g$ and $p$ refer to gas and particle phases, respectively. Note that the particle–particle interaction terms are not included in the above equations. The justification for this omission as well as the effect of particle–particle interactions on propulsive performance are discussed in Sec. V. Here $\boldsymbol{M}_{prop \rightarrow g}$ and $\boldsymbol{M}_{prop \rightarrow p}$ represent momentum source terms for the gas phase and particle phase, respectively, due to regression. The shear stress in the gas phase is modeled as follows:

$$ \tau_g = \mu_g (\nabla \boldsymbol{u}_g + \nabla \boldsymbol{u}_g^T) - \frac{2}{3} \mu_g (\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{u}_g) \boldsymbol{I} \tag{4} $$

The energy conservation equations for the gas phase and particle phase considered are:

$$ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\alpha_g \rho_g h_g) + \nabla \cdot (\alpha_g \rho_g \boldsymbol{u}_g h_g) = \nabla \cdot \left( \frac{\alpha_g \lambda_g}{C_{p,g}} \nabla h_g \right) + \alpha_g \frac{DP}{Dt} & + \alpha_g \boldsymbol{\tau}_g : \nabla \boldsymbol{u}_g + \dot{q}'''_{p \rightarrow g} \\ & + f_{D,g} \cdot (\boldsymbol{u}_p - \boldsymbol{u}_g) + H_{prop \rightarrow g} \tag{5} \end{aligned} $$

$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(\alpha_p \rho_p h_p) + \nabla \cdot (\alpha_p \rho_p \boldsymbol{u}_p h_p) = \alpha_p \frac{DP}{Dt} + H_{prop \rightarrow p} + \dot{q}'''_{g \rightarrow p} \tag{6} $$

where $h$ is the enthalpy, $\lambda$ is the thermal conductivity, $C_p$ is the specific heat, $H_{\text{prop}\to k}$ is the enthalpy source term for the $k$th phase due to regression, and $q'''_{i\to j}$ is the rate of heat transfer from the $i$th phase to the $j$th phase. Note that the source terms due to propellant regression are only relevant to the computational cells adjacent to the surface of the propellant bed. For the remaining computational cells inside the rocket motor, only the source terms arising from particle–gas interactions are relevant.
